The Structure Beneath the Number:
What a Single Mortality Assumption Hides

Mortality is the most material — and most casually compressed — assumption in a life or annuity portfolio. This briefing takes it apart, exhibit by exhibit, and shows where the compression turns into risk.

SUMMARY

Executive summary

In practice, mortality enters a valuation model as one base table multiplied by one improvement scalar. That reduction is convenient and, for a first-order reserve, defensible. It is also where basis risk is born. The features a blended assumption smooths away — the young-adult accident hump, the post-2011 improvement stall, the compression of the survival curve, and the deceleration of mortality in the tail — do not disappear; they re-emerge later as adverse actual-to-expected experience, mis-priced options, and capital that is held against the wrong shape of risk.

Headline metrics recompute live from the selected basis and valuation year. Move the controls at the top of the page to re-run every exhibit on a different table.

  • Youth mortality is not monotone. An accident hump of predominantly external-cause deaths lifts young-adult qx some 30–50% above the smooth senescent trend. A Gompertz-only base table prices it away and systematically under-loads term and group life at younger ages.
  • Longevity improvement is neither smooth nor guaranteed. US period life expectancy plateaued around 2011 — before COVID — and then fell roughly 1.8 years in 2020–21, the sharpest reversal since the Second World War. A flat 1.0–1.5%/yr improvement scale is an optimistic extrapolation, not a neutral one.
  • Improvement is heterogeneous by age. Infant qx fell 3–4%/yr for decades while working-age mortality barely moved and, recently, turned negative. A single portfolio-wide improvement rate averages away exactly the structure that drives A/E variance.
  • The survival curve is rectangularising. Deaths are compressing into a narrowing band around a rising modal age. The risk is migrating from the level of mortality to the shape of its distribution — which matters more for annuity tail reserves than the mean does.
  • Mortality decelerates in the tail. Above roughly age 95 the death rate bends below the Gompertz line toward a plateau. Straight-line extrapolation overstates centenarian mortality and can under-value deep-deferred annuity and longevity-swap liabilities.
  • The trend is national, not universal. Peer countries kept improving while the US stalled; Japan and South Korea show what sustained improvement looks like, while Russia shows it can reverse for a generation. Any assumption imported from one population silently imports that country's idiosyncratic history.
I

The anatomy of the mortality curve

Human mortality is not one process but three, superimposed. Reading them separately is the difference between a table that fits and a table that explains.

The Heligman–Pollard law1 decomposes the death rate into three additive regimes: a childhood term that falls steeply from infancy; an accident hump that rises and falls across young adulthood; and a senescent term — the familiar Gompertz exponential — that dominates from mid-life onward. Plotted on a logarithmic scale (Exhibit 1), the total curve is the visible sum; the three coloured components are what actually generate it.

The feature most single-curve fits erase is the hump. Between roughly ages 18 and 27 the death rate briefly stops falling and reverses — a bulge of external-cause mortality (accidents, suicide, homicide) that has nothing to do with senescence and everything to do with behaviour. It is small in absolute terms but proportionally large: at its peak it can add 30–50% to qx relative to the underlying trend, and it is overwhelmingly male.

Reading the components separately is not an academic nicety — it changes the graduation. Each term has a distinct cause behind it: the childhood term reflects congenital and perinatal risk that medicine has steadily compressed; the hump reflects the collision of physical maturity with immature judgement and risk-taking; the senescent term reflects the exponential accumulation of biological damage that no population has yet escaped. Because the three move for different reasons and at different speeds, a table that models them as one smooth curve will mis-track whenever a single component shifts on its own — exactly what happened when the accident hump swelled through the opioid epidemic while senescent mortality carried on falling. The Heligman–Pollard parameters give the actuary a handle on each mechanism independently, so that a change in young-adult behaviour and a change in old-age medicine are not forced to share one dial.

1.1The eight parameters and what each one governs

The value of a structural law is that its parameters are not free-floating curve-fitting knobs; each maps to a demographic mechanism an actuary can reason about and stress independently. That interpretability is the whole point of preferring Heligman–Pollard to an unstructured spline.

TermParameterGoverns
ChildhoodA, B, Cthe level of infant mortality (A), the location of the childhood decline (B), and the speed at which child mortality falls toward its minimum (C).
Accident humpD, E, Fthe severity/height of the young-adult bump (D), its spread or width (E), and the modal age at which it peaks (F, typically 18–22).
SenescenceG, Hthe baseline level of old-age mortality (G) and its rate of increase with age (H) — where H sets the mortality "doubling time," roughly ln 2 ⁄ ln H ≈ 8 years in developed populations.

Because each mechanism has its own dial, an actuary can ask targeted questions the single-curve fit cannot answer: what happens to reserves if the hump (D) doubles under an opioid-style shock but senescence (G, H) is unchanged? — or if the doubling time H lengthens as old-age medicine improves? The parameters localise the assumption to the biology it represents.

Gompertz–Makeham the classical special case in which mortality is a constant "background" risk (Makeham's term, independent of age) plus an exponential senescent term, μx = A + B·cx. Heligman–Pollard is essentially this senescent core with two extra pieces bolted on — the childhood decline and the accident hump — to make it fit the full age range rather than just adult ages, where Gompertz alone is excellent.

1.2Why a fitted curve still demands judgement

Fitting the law to raw experience is not the end of the job. Crude death rates at individual ages are noisy — especially in a small portfolio or at very old ages where exposure is thin — so the fitted curve must be graduated: smoothed to remove sampling noise while preserving genuine shape. Over-smoothing flattens the very hump this section is about; under-smoothing lets random spikes masquerade as structure. The tension between fidelity and smoothness is a matter of actuarial judgement, not arithmetic.

There is a deeper caveat for insured work. A population table reflects everyone; an insured population is self-selected — the medically underwritten are healthier than average at outset (the "selection effect"), an advantage that wears off over the first several policy years (the "duration effect"). A curve fitted to national data therefore mis-states insured mortality until it is adjusted for selection, and pension-scheme lives differ again by socioeconomic mix. The shape of the curve is universal; its level is population-specific.

Actuarial implication — pricing A base table graduated only to the senescent trend will be biased low precisely where young lives are underwritten. For term assurance and group life the hump is a genuine loading, not noise; for individual annuities it is immaterial. The same population table therefore carries opposite pricing errors depending on the product — which is why a base table should be selected against the exposure it will actually price, not adopted wholesale.
II

The longevity trajectory — and its reversals

The industry's working assumption for a century has been that people keep living longer at a roughly steady pace. The record is less reassuring than the assumption.

Two things are visible in Exhibit 2 that a constant-improvement assumption cannot produce. First, the curve bends flat around 2011 — a genuine slowdown in mortality improvement across most developed markets, documented in successive revisions of the SOA and CMI improvement scales2, and driven in the US partly by stagnating working-age mortality. Second, the 2020–21 collapse: a fall of roughly 1.8 years, the largest short-run reversal in the post-war record. Neither is captured by extrapolating the 1980–2010 trend.

Exhibit 3 reframes the same improvement as a change in shape. As deaths at young and middle ages are eliminated, the survival curve becomes more rectangular3: almost everyone now reaches old age, and deaths pile up in a narrowing window. The mean rises, but the more important actuarial fact is that the dispersion of the age at death is shrinking — longevity risk is concentrating into the tail rather than spreading across it.

A further subtlety sits beneath the headline number, and it trips up non-specialists constantly. What is plotted here is period life expectancy — the figure obtained by applying a single calendar year's death rates across a hypothetical lifetime, as if mortality never improved again. It is the standard reporting basis, but it is deliberately pessimistic: a real newborn belongs to a cohort that will enjoy decades of future improvement, so its realised lifespan is typically several years longer than the period figure suggests. The gap between period and cohort expectancy is not a rounding detail — it is itself an assumption about future improvement, and quoting one where the other belongs is among the more common and expensive errors in longevity work. An annuity priced on period life expectancy, unadjusted, is priced to be too cheap.

The reversals matter for a reason beyond their size. Because reserves are set years in advance on a best-estimate path, a stall or shock does not merely dent one year's result — it invalidates the trend that the whole liability was projected along. The 2011 plateau and the pandemic together closed the era in which "mortality always improves at a steady pace" could be treated as a background fact rather than a live, contestable assumption.

2.1Period versus cohort, quantified

The abstract distinction becomes concrete once you put numbers on it. Take a 65-year-old today. On a period basis we apply this year's mortality at 66, 67, 68 … and read off a remaining expectation. But that person will actually experience the future mortality of ages 66, 67, 68 — each lower than today's if improvement continues. Discounting in even a modest improvement trend adds materially to expected lifespan.

Worked illustration — the cohort uplift Suppose period e65 = 17.3 years (the male figure on this model) and mortality improves at a steady 1.25%/yr at every age. Allowing each future year's rates to fall accordingly typically lifts the cohort expectation by roughly 1.5–2.5 years — an annuity that ignores it is under-reserved by a comparable proportion of the tail. The uplift is not a prudential margin you can choose to omit; it is the difference between two correct answers to two different questions.

2.2Anatomy of the 2011 stall

The plateau visible in Exhibit 2 was not a statistical artefact. Across the UK, US and much of Europe, the annualised improvement rate roughly halved after about 2011 — from the ~2–3%/yr enjoyed in the 2000s to well under 1%. The causes are still debated and probably plural: the exhaustion of easy cardiovascular gains (the great twentieth-century engine of improvement), austerity and health-system pressure, worsening obesity and metabolic disease, and — in the US specifically — the deaths-of-despair reversal among working-age adults. Whatever the mix, the actuarial consequence is singular: the trend that improvement models had extrapolated for two decades broke, and reserves calibrated to it were, in hindsight, optimistic.

2.3Rectangularisation and the compression of mortality

Exhibit 3's changing shape has a name and a measurable signature. As the survival curve squares off, the modal age at death (the peak of the deaths curve) rises while the dispersion of ages at death around it contracts — the phenomenon Fries called the "compression of mortality." One clean summary statistic is the standard deviation of age at death above the mode, or the interquartile range shown in the Overview metrics: both have fallen over the century. For an annuity writer this is double-edged — a higher modal age raises the mean liability, but tighter dispersion reduces the diversification across ages at death, concentrating the portfolio's longevity risk into a narrower, more correlated band.

Modal age at death the single most common age of death in a population — the peak of the dx curve, ignoring infant deaths. Unlike the mean, it is insensitive to the long left tail of early deaths, and demographers increasingly track it as the cleaner marker of how far the frontier of old age has moved.
Actuarial implication — reserving & capital Choice of improvement scale is now a first-order reserving decision, not a technical footnote. A flat 1.5%/yr assumption is inconsistent with the observed post-2011 path and embeds optimism into annuity reserves. Under a Solvency II-style framework, the shift from level to shape also matters: the 1-in-200 longevity stress is increasingly a trend-and-tail risk (people living to concentrated older ages) rather than a level shift, and should be calibrated to the distribution, not the mean.
III

The engine of reserves: mortality improvement

For an in-force block, the present value of liabilities is far more sensitive to the rate of change of mortality than to its current level. That rate is neither constant across ages nor stable over time.

The heat-map makes the heterogeneity impossible to average away. Infant and child mortality improved at 3–4% per year for much of the twentieth century — the single largest contributor to twentieth-century longevity gains. Old-age improvement, by contrast, arrived late and modestly. Most striking for a pricing actuary is the working-age band (15–50) turning red in the 2010s: the US "deaths of despair" phenomenon4 — drug poisoning, alcohol, suicide — pushed prime-age mortality up, a reversal with no precedent in the peacetime record.

Why does the rate of change dominate reserves so completely? Because improvement compounds. A 1.5% annual reduction in mortality does almost nothing in year one, but sustained across the thirty-year horizon of an annuity it lowers the death rate by more than a third — and every survivor it creates draws income for longer. Small differences in the assumed rate therefore fan out into large differences in liability, which is precisely why the age-by-age structure in Exhibit 4, rather than the current level of mortality, is where the balance-sheet sensitivity actually lives. Two portfolios with identical present mortality but different assumed improvement can carry reserves that differ by double-digit percentages.

There is a second dimension the heat-map only hints at. Improvement is not purely a property of age in a given year; it also travels with year of birth. Particular cohorts carry unusually fast — or slow — improvement with them throughout life, the best-documented example being Britain's 1925–45 "golden cohort," whose members improved faster than both the generations before and after them for reasons still debated. Properly, then, improvement is a surface over age and cohort, which is why modern projection bases (CMI, SOA MP-series) are two-dimensional rather than a single scalar applied uniformly.

3.1How improvement compounds — the arithmetic

The claim that "the rate matters more than the level" is not rhetorical; it falls straight out of compounding. A constant annual reduction r applied for t years multiplies the death rate by (1−r)t. Small rates, long horizons, large effects:

After 30 years Book B's assumed death rates are about 14% lower than Book A's (0.55· vs 0.635 of the starting level). Because survivors then draw income for longer, that feeds through to a reserve difference in the high single digits to low double digits of percent on a deferred annuity — from a half-point difference in a single assumption that never touches the current table.

3.2The two-dimensional structure of improvement

Exhibit 4 slices improvement by age and calendar period, but a full model needs a third view: year of birth. Plotted on a Lexis surface (age × year), improvement shows diagonal ridges — cohorts carrying their own momentum through life. Modern projection frameworks encode all three:

Reduction factor / improvement scale the multiplier applied to a base table to project it forward: qx,t+1 = qx,t · (1 − RFx,t). In the SOA MP-series and the UK CMI model the factor RF varies by both age and calendar year (and blends toward a long-term rate), rather than being a single constant — precisely to capture the heterogeneity this section documents.

3.3What actually moves the rate

Improvement is not weather; it has causes an actuary should be able to name when defending an assumption. The historical drivers, in rough chronological order of their contribution, were: public health (sanitation, clean water, vaccination) which crushed infectious and child mortality; the cardiovascular revolution (smoking decline, blood-pressure and cholesterol control, acute cardiac care) which drove the mid-to-late-century adult gains; and, more marginally so far, oncology and the slow chipping-away at cancer mortality. The forward question — which is the assumption-setting question — is whether any engine of comparable power (metabolic disease, dementia, ageing biology itself) is next, or whether the frontier simply advances more slowly from here.

Actuarial implication — experience analysis A single blended improvement rate applied across a portfolio will produce structured A/E residuals: consistently over-reserving where improvement is fast and under-reserving where it has stalled. Improvement should be modelled by age (and ideally cohort) — via a two-dimensional scale (SOA MP-series, CMI) or a stochastic projection (Lee–Carter, CBD5) — and experience studies should test residuals by age band, not just in aggregate, where offsetting biases hide.
IV

Decomposing the gains: which ages bought the years

A rise in life expectancy is a single number standing in for thousands of age-specific changes. Attributing it back to the ages that produced it reveals how the source of longevity has migrated up the age scale — and why future gains are harder to win.

Exhibit 5 applies the standard Arriaga decomposition7, which splits the change in life expectancy at birth between the selected year and 1950 into the exact contribution of each age band. The arithmetic is exact: the bars sum to the total change in e0 shown beneath them.

The pattern is the central fact of twentieth-century longevity. Early gains were overwhelmingly bought at young ages — the near-elimination of infant and child mortality did most of the work and is, by construction, close to exhausted. Continued improvement must therefore come from older ages, where each year of life expectancy is far more expensive to produce. When the working-age bands turn negative in recent decompositions, they are actively subtracting from the total — the quantitative signature of the deaths-of-despair reversal.

The force behind this upward migration is the epidemiological transition: as a society conquers infectious and childhood disease, deaths shift to later life and to chronic, degenerative causes. Once that transition is largely complete — as it is across the developed world — the cheap, high-yield interventions that once saved children have no equivalent at older ages, where mortality is a tangle of cancer, cardiovascular and neurodegenerative disease that yields only slowly and expensively. The decomposition is, in effect, the actuarial fingerprint of that transition: the bars march up the age axis decade by decade, and the total they sum to becomes progressively harder to grow. It is the clearest single answer to the question "why should we not simply extrapolate the last century's improvement?"

4.1How to read the decomposition

Arriaga's method answers a precise question: of the total change in life expectancy between two tables, how many years came from mortality change in each age band? It does so by splitting each band's contribution into a direct effect (fewer deaths within the band add life-years lived in it) and an indirect effect (survivors saved in the band go on to live through all later ages too). The arithmetic is exact and additive, which is why the bars sum precisely to the headline change in e0 — no residual, no overlap. Slide the valuation year and watch the mass of the contribution migrate from the youngest bands (dominant for 1950→1980) toward the oldest (dominant for recent decades).

4.2The epidemiological transition, in stages

The upward migration follows a well-mapped sequence, first set out by Omran and since extended:

  1. Age of pestilence and famine. Mortality high and volatile; life expectancy 20–40; infectious disease and famine dominate; most deaths are children.
  2. Age of receding pandemics. Sanitation, nutrition and vaccination cut infectious mortality; child survival soars; life expectancy climbs into the 50s — the steep phase the emerging economies are on now.
  3. Age of degenerative & man-made disease. Deaths shift to heart disease, stroke and cancer at older ages; improvement slows and becomes a matter of managing chronic illness.
  4. Age of delayed degenerative disease. Medicine pushes those same chronic deaths to ever-later ages — the frontier developed world, where each additional year is bought at the oldest ages and the highest cost.

The decomposition bars are simply this narrative rendered numerically: a country's development stage is legible from which age bands are doing the work.

4.3Why old-age gains are structurally dearer

Saving a child from measles returns 60–70 life-years for a cheap, one-off intervention. Postponing a cardiovascular death at 80 might return two or three years and requires sustained, expensive management. As mortality concentrates at ages where the residual life expectancy is small, the life-years bought per unit of medical progress collapse — so even continued medical advance yields ever-smaller life-expectancy gains. This diminishing return is the quantitative heart of the improvement-slowdown debate, and it is a reason to expect slower gains even without assuming medicine stalls.

Actuarial implication — assumption setting Because the reservoir of easy young-age gains is drained, future improvement is structurally slower and more uncertain than the historical average implies. Extrapolating a long-run trend that was powered by child mortality into a future that depends on old-age medicine is a category error — it borrows optimism from a source that no longer exists. Improvement assumptions for an annuity book should be anchored to old-age experience, not to the headline century-long trend.
V

Composition and timing of death

Two structural details rarely reach the pricing basis: what people die of changes completely across the lifespan, and when in the year they die is far from uniform.

Exhibit 6 shows that for lives under about 40, external causes dominate — mortality there is a behavioural and social exposure, not a medical one. Circulatory disease and cancer only take over in mid-life, and their shares themselves shift with medical progress. The underwriting consequence is direct: assessing a young applicant purely on medical factors measures the wrong risk, while the same factors are decisive at older ages. It also means cause-specific shocks land unevenly across a book — an opioid trend hits a young protection portfolio and barely touches an annuity book.

Exhibit 7 shows the seasonal signature: mortality runs roughly 20% above trend in January and below it through summer, driven by respiratory and circulatory deaths among the old. Annualised rates wash this out entirely. It matters for short-horizon exposure — the timing of claims within a valuation period, the design of seasonal reinsurance triggers, and pandemic-season stress testing, where a winter respiratory shock compounds an already elevated baseline.

Read together, the two exhibits describe a moving target governed by competing risks. The cause mix is not fixed: whenever one cause recedes, the people it would have killed survive to die of something else, so progress against heart disease mechanically raises the share of cancer and dementia — not because those diseases became more common, but because more people now live long enough to reach them. For pricing this carries a hard lesson: a cause-specific shock can never be read in isolation. Eliminating a cause frees its would-be victims for the next one, so the net effect on total mortality is always smaller than the gross effect on the headline cause — and a medical breakthrough that looks transformative for one disease can move all-cause mortality surprisingly little.

5.1The cause transition across a single life

Exhibit 6 is really a portrait of changing risk type. In infancy, mortality is congenital and perinatal. Through childhood and into young adulthood it becomes overwhelmingly external — the accident hump of Section I is this same fact viewed by cause rather than by rate. From the forties, circulatory disease and cancer climb steeply and dominate the rest of life, with respiratory and infectious deaths rising again at the very end as frailty sets in. The practical reading: the information that predicts death is different at every age. A cholesterol reading is near-useless for a 22-year-old and decisive for a 62-year-old; a question about driving, drinking or mental health reverses that ordering.

5.2Competing risks, quantified

Worked illustration — curing a disease moves the total less than you think Suppose at age 70 the annual death probability is q = 0.020, of which circulatory disease accounts for 30% (0.006). Imagine we eliminate circulatory death entirely at that age. Naïvely, q falls to 0.014 — a 30% cut. But those survivors remain exposed to every other cause next year and thereafter; over a few years a large share die of cancer, respiratory or other disease instead. The lasting gain in life expectancy is therefore much smaller than "30% fewer deaths this year" suggests — often less than half of it, once competing causes reclaim the survivors. This is why headline "cure" figures rarely translate into proportionate longevity gains.

5.3Why winter kills

The seasonal signature in Exhibit 7 is not one effect but several stacked in the same months: circulating influenza and respiratory viruses; cold-triggered increases in blood pressure and clotting that raise cardiovascular events; and, in some populations, cold-housing and fuel poverty among the frail elderly. The burden falls almost entirely on the old and the already-sick, which is why the seasonal swing is far larger for over-75 mortality than for the working ages. For the pricing actuary the lesson is about timing risk within a valuation period: a book's claims are not uniform through the year, and a severe influenza season or a winter pandemic wave lands on an already-elevated baseline — the scenario reinsurance triggers and mortality-catastrophe covers are written against.

VI

Differentials and the tail

Two effects sit at the edges of the table where intuition is weakest — the sex differential, which peaks young rather than old, and the behaviour of mortality at the extreme of the human lifespan.

The gender gap (Exhibit 8) is widely assumed to be an old-age phenomenon. It is the opposite: the ratio peaks at 2.5–3× in young adulthood, driven almost entirely by the male accident hump, and then narrows steadily into the nineties as senescent causes — which are more sex-symmetric — take over. Unisex pricing therefore mis-states risk most severely on young male protection, and the direction of the error flips at older ages.

In the tail (Exhibit 9) the Gompertz law over-predicts. Above roughly age 95 the observed death rate bends below the exponential toward a plateau — a deceleration confirmed in large centenarian datasets6 and attributed to mortality selection and biological heterogeneity. Naïvely extrapolating a Gompertz fit inflates qx past 100, which understates the reserve for deep-deferred annuities, whole-of-life at extreme ages, and any longevity-swap cash flows concentrated in the tail.

The deceleration has a clean statistical explanation worth stating, because it shows the failure is conceptual rather than numerical. Any population is a mixture of robust and frail individuals — call it frailty selection. As the population ages, the frail die first, so the survivors at extreme ages are an increasingly select, hardy subgroup whose death rate rises more slowly than the original mixed cohort's would have. The Gompertz law assumes a homogeneous population and therefore cannot see this; it keeps extrapolating a rate that, by age 105, applies to essentially no one still alive. It is a textbook case of a model breaking not because its mathematics are wrong but because its population assumption has quietly stopped holding — the same species of error, at the opposite end of the age range, as ignoring the accident hump.

6.1Why the gender gap peaks in youth

The gap is the sum of a behavioural component and a biological one, and they peak at opposite ends of life. In young adulthood the behavioural component dominates: the male accident hump — risk-taking, violence, accidents — has little female counterpart, driving the ratio to 2.5–3×. From mid-life the biological component takes over: men suffer earlier cardiovascular disease and carry the legacy of historically higher smoking, but these differences are proportionally smaller against a large senescent base, so the ratio narrows even as the absolute gap in deaths persists. As historical smoking and drinking differences between the sexes converge, the mid-life portion of the gap has been slowly closing — a genuine, if gradual, trend an assumption should reflect.

6.2Frailty and the plateau

Section VI's tail exhibit rests on heterogeneity in frailty. Model each person as carrying a fixed "frailty" multiplier on the population baseline; the frail die younger, so the survivors at 100 are drawn disproportionately from the robust tail of the original distribution. Their observed death rate therefore climbs more slowly than any single individual's true hazard — the population rate decelerates even while every individual's risk keeps compounding. This is not a curiosity: whether the late-life hazard truly plateaus, keeps rising slowly, or even declines is one of the live debates in demography, and it swings the value of extreme-age liabilities.

Frailty selection the mechanism by which a heterogeneous population's observed mortality rate rises more slowly than any individual's, because higher-risk members are progressively removed, leaving an ever-more-select survivor pool. It makes population-level laws (Gompertz) systematically over-state mortality at the oldest ages.

6.3Closing the table

Because data above age ~95 is sparse and noisy, actuaries "close" a table by imposing a functional form on the extreme ages rather than trusting raw rates. The common choices differ precisely in how much late-life deceleration they build in:

MethodBehaviour at extreme age
Gompertz extensionpure exponential — no deceleration; highest tail mortality (over-states, per this section).
Coale–Kiskerrate of increase declines linearly above a pivot age — moderate deceleration.
Kannisto (logistic)hazard flattens toward a plateau — strong deceleration; widely used for annuities.

The choice is material: for a portfolio with meaningful exposure past 100, a logistic close and a Gompertz close can differ by years of tail life expectancy, and therefore by a noticeable slice of reserve.

Actuarial implication — model risk Both effects are places where a smooth parametric law is wrong in a predictable direction. The remedy is not a better single curve but explicit treatment: retain the hump for protection bases, close the table with a decelerating (not exponential) tail for annuity bases, and hold the difference as an identified model-risk margin rather than an unquantified prudence.
VII

An international perspective

The US path is not the human path. Placed alongside its peers, every idiosyncrasy of a national table — a stall, a collapse, a miracle of catch-up — becomes visible, and with it the danger of importing one country's experience into another's basis.

Four stories dominate the chart. Japan and, even more dramatically, South Korea show sustained, decades-long improvement that carried them from mid-century poverty to the top of the global table. The United States flattens conspicuously after 2010 and falls furthest in the pandemic — the peer-group laggard. Russia is the cautionary extreme: an outright collapse in the 1990s, with male life expectancy falling to the fifties, followed by a partial recovery. And the emerging economies — China, India, Nigeria — trace the steep early-gain curve the West climbed a century earlier, still powered by falling child mortality.

The demographer's expectation was convergence: as knowledge and medicine spread, laggard countries would catch up and the whole world would bunch toward a common frontier. The chart shows that convergence is real but incomplete and reversible. Countries at the frontier improve slowly because they have already banked the easy gains; countries far behind can improve explosively, as Korea did, by importing solutions wholesale. But institutions, diet, inequality and public-health policy drive persistent, decades-long divergences between otherwise similar nations — and, as Russia and the recent United States show, a country can fall off the frontier entirely when its social fabric or its risk environment deteriorates. There is no gravitational law pulling every population to the same life expectancy.

To make the drivers concrete, the panel below collects the perspectives of demographers, health economists and actuaries in nine countries. Each speaks to what has moved mortality in their own population — and what now threatens or sustains it.

But we are now the frontier of ageing: improvement has slowed to a crawl, and the question is no longer how to add years but how to compress disability into the very end of a very long life.

7.1Four archetypes of national mortality

Behind nine countries sit essentially four patterns, and recognising which one a portfolio's population follows tells you more about its future than any single current rate.

ArchetypeExamplesSignature & outlook
Frontier, slowingJapan, FranceAlready at the top; improvement now slow and hard-won; risk is a further stall, not collapse.
Explosive catch-upSouth Korea, ChinaRapid convergence from a low base as solutions are imported wholesale; gains eventually decelerate as they meet the frontier.

The archetypes are not permanent. Russia shows a country can fall from one to another — a frontier-adjacent economy that reverted under social collapse — and the United States is the cautionary example of a frontier nation drifting into the "faltering" class. An improvement assumption is, implicitly, a bet on which archetype the population will occupy over the life of the liability.

Actuarial implication — basis risk & longevity transfer Cross-border reinsurance and longevity-swap portfolios are exposed to exactly this divergence. A reference table borrowed from one country embeds its national trajectory; hedging a UK book against a US index, or reserving an emerging-market portfolio on a developed-market improvement scale, introduces population basis risk that is invisible in the mean and only surfaces when the two countries' paths separate — as, repeatedly, they have.
VIII

Projection and sensitivity

Every reserve is a bet on a mortality path that has not happened yet. The point of a sensitivity exhibit is not to predict it but to show how wide the plausible fan is — and therefore how much rides on the improvement assumption alone.

Exhibit 11 takes the currently selected life table as its jump-off point and projects period life expectancy forward to 2065 under four flat improvement assumptions: a stall (0%), and constant 1.0%, 1.5% and 2.0% per year applied uniformly across ages. The spread between them by mid-century is several years of life expectancy — and, for a book of annuities, several percent of reserves.

The exhibit is deliberately naïve — a flat scalar is precisely the assumption this briefing argues against — but that is the point. It brackets the answer and makes the assumption risk legible. A rigorous basis would replace the flat scalars with a two-dimensional (age × time) scale or a stochastic model that generates a full distribution of paths, from which a 1-in-200 stress can be read directly rather than guessed.

The distinction between the two approaches is practical, not pedantic. A deterministic projection like this one answers "what if improvement runs at X%?" — invaluable for framing and for regulatory sensitivity tests, but silent on how likely any given path is. A stochastic model (Lee–Carter, CBD and their descendants) instead simulates thousands of plausible mortality futures and returns a full probability distribution, so that reserves, risk margins and the 1-in-200 capital stress emerge as percentiles rather than as hand-picked assumptions. The fan opposite is the deterministic sketch that makes the risk visible; a capital model needs the stochastic engine sitting behind it.

Notice, too, that the jump-off point moves with the valuation-year control. Projecting from a pandemic-depressed table (2021) versus a pre-pandemic one (2019) produces materially different forward paths from the same scenario assumptions — a live illustration of jump-off risk: the base year you happen to reserve from is itself a modelling choice, and an unusually good or bad recent year can bias a projection for decades.

8.1From the fan to a capital number

The spread between the scenarios is not just illustrative; it is the raw material of a longevity capital charge. A regulator's 1-in-200 longevity stress asks: how much would reserves rise under a mortality path so favourable to annuitants that only a 1-in-200 year would beat it? A deterministic fan brackets the answer crudely; a stochastic model reads it off directly as a percentile of simulated paths. Either way the mechanism is the one shown here — a permanent shift in the improvement trend, compounded over the whole run-off of the book.

Worked illustration — trend risk dwarfs a single-year shock A one-off pandemic that raises one year's deaths by 10% is painful but transient — it hits a single year's result and is gone. A permanent 0.5%/yr increase in the improvement trend is, for an annuity book, far larger in present-value terms, because it lowers every future year's mortality and lengthens every survivor's payout stream. This is why longevity capital is dominated by trend risk, while mortality (protection) capital worries more about level and catastrophe risk — the same balance sheet, opposite tails.

8.2Jump-off risk in one control

Move the valuation-year slider from 2019 to 2021 and watch all four projected paths drop in parallel. Nothing about the future assumptions changed — only the base year the projection starts from. Reserving off a pandemic-depressed table hard-wires that depression into every future year unless it is explicitly treated as temporary; reserving off an unusually benign year does the opposite. Choosing — and, where necessary, normalising — the jump-off table is a modelling decision in its own right, and one an unusually good or bad recent year can quietly distort for decades.

Actuarial implication — capital & ALM The width of this fan is the longevity trend risk the balance sheet carries. It should be quantified, disclosed and hedged as a distribution, not collapsed to a single best-estimate line with an unexamined margin. Where the exposure is material — a large annuity or pension book — the case for longevity risk transfer rests on precisely the uncertainty this exhibit makes visible.
IX

Life-table extract

The exhibits above all derive from one underlying life table. A selection of its columns is shown below and recomputes with the controls.

The columns are not independent — each is built from the one before it, which is what makes a life table a complete, self-contained model of a population's mortality rather than a loose collection of statistics. Begin with qx, the probability of dying within the year. Apply it to the survivors lx to get the year's deaths dx; subtract those to carry survivors forward to the next age; accumulate the years of life those survivors collectively live, and divide back by lx to obtain ex, the expectation of life remaining. Every chart in this briefing — the survival curve, the decomposition, the projections — is ultimately a different view of this one object, which is why they can never disagree with one another. Reading across a single row shows the state of a synthetic cohort at that age; reading down a column shows how a quantity evolves across the whole lifespan.

Age xqxper 100klxdxex (yrs)

qx = probability of death within the year · lx = survivors from 100,000 births · dx = deaths in the year · ex = complete expectation of life remaining. Infant a0 ≈ 0.10 (early clustering of neonatal deaths) rather than the naïve 0.5.

9.1Two rates that are easily confused

Newcomers routinely conflate the two ways of expressing mortality, and mixing them corrupts a table. The initial rate qx is a probability — deaths in the year divided by the lives alive at its start. The central rate mx is a rate — deaths divided by the average population exposed during the year (person-years lived). They are related by qx = mx ⁄ (1 + (1−ax)·mx) and are close at young ages but diverge where mortality is high. Published data usually gives central rates; life-table probabilities must be derived from them, and getting the conversion wrong biases the whole table.

qx vs mx qx is the probability a life aged exactly x dies before x+1; mx is the death rate per person-year of exposure across the year. Use qx to build the survivorship column; mx is what raw experience and national statistics typically report.

9.2The a₀ adjustment, and why 0.5 is wrong

The ax factor is the average fraction of the final year lived by those who die in it. For most ages, deaths fall roughly mid-year and ax ≈ 0.5 is fine. Infancy is the glaring exception: neonatal deaths cluster in the first days and weeks of life, so infants who die live on average only about a tenth of the year — hence a0 ≈ 0.1. Using the naïve 0.5 credits dying infants with far too much life lived, over-stating L0 and inflating life expectancy at birth by a few tenths of a year. It is a small, standard correction that a surprising amount of casual analysis gets wrong — and a clean example of how a life table's accuracy lives in its details.

Conclusions & recommendations

Mortality rewards the actuary who respects its structure and punishes the one who averages it away. The recurring lesson of the exhibits above is that the risk lives in the features a single assumption smooths over — and that those features are knowable in advance.

A base table and an improvement scalar remain the right starting point. But each of the effects documented here — the accident hump, the age-heterogeneity of improvement, the post-2011 slowdown, rectangularisation, late-life deceleration and the national divergence of trends — is a place where that starting point is wrong in a known direction. Left unmodelled they do not average out; they accumulate as basis risk, surface as adverse actual-to-expected experience, and misdirect capital toward the wrong shape of risk. The following actions translate the analysis into practice.

  • Select the base table against the actual exposure. Retain the young-adult accident hump for protection and group-life bases; it is immaterial for annuities but a genuine loading for term.
  • Model improvement in two dimensions. Replace any single portfolio-wide scalar with an age-×-time scale (SOA MP-series / CMI) or a stochastic projection (Lee–Carter, CBD), and anchor old-age improvement to old-age experience rather than the century-long headline trend.
  • Test experience by age band, not just in aggregate. Offsetting biases hide in the total; structured A/E residuals are the diagnostic that a blended assumption is failing.
  • Close annuity tables with a decelerating tail. Do not extrapolate Gompertz past ~95; hold the difference as an identified model-risk margin rather than unquantified prudence.
  • Treat longevity trend as a distribution. Quantify, disclose and — where material — hedge the improvement fan shown in Exhibit 11 as a range of paths, reading the 1-in-200 stress from the distribution.
  • Name the population basis risk in cross-border deals. A borrowed national table imports a national history; make that assumption explicit in any reinsurance or longevity-swap arrangement spanning countries.

10.1A hierarchy of refinements

Not every book warrants every refinement. The right response is proportionate — matched to the materiality of the mortality exposure and the direction of the risk. A workable ladder, from minimum to best practice:

LevelPracticeSuited to
BaselineStandard base table + single improvement scalarSmall or immaterial blocks; first-order estimates.
StructuredProduct-appropriate base (hump retained or tail closed) + two-dimensional (age × time) improvement scaleAny material protection or annuity book.
StochasticSimulated mortality distribution (Lee–Carter / CBD); percentile-based reserves and capitalLarge annuity/pension blocks; internal capital models.
ManagedThe above + experience monitoring by age band, jump-off normalisation, and longevity risk transfer where warrantedBooks where longevity is a principal, disclosed risk.

The point of the ladder is that each rung neutralises a specific distortion documented in this briefing — the hump, the age-heterogeneity of improvement, the tail deceleration, jump-off and trend risk. Climbing it is not gold-plating; it is converting known, directional model errors into explicit, quantified margins.

Bottom line None of this argues for abandoning the simple assumption — only for knowing precisely where and by how much it is wrong, and holding that knowledge as an explicit, quantified margin. The single number is a summary, not the risk. The risk is in its structure.

Methodology & technical notes

Every exhibit is generated from a single, internally consistent mortality surface, so no two charts can contradict one another. Death rates are produced by an eight-parameter Heligman–Pollard law calibrated so that the 2019 basis reproduces US-style period life expectancy (male e0 ≈ 76.3 / e65 ≈ 17.3; female e0 ≈ 81.4 / e65 ≈ 20.8):

qx / px = A(x+B)C  +  D · exp(−E · [ln(x/F)]²)  +  G · Hx

The three terms are the childhood, accident-hump and senescent components plotted in Exhibit 1. The full 1950–2023 surface is built by applying age-specific annual improvement to the 2019 anchor, incorporating (i) faster mid-century gains, (ii) the documented post-2011 slowdown, (iii) a working-age reversal in the 2010s, and (iv) an age-skewed 2020–21 pandemic shock. Life-table functions (lx, dx, Lx, Tx, ex) use standard recursions with an a0 ≈ 0.10 infant adjustment; the naïve 0.5 alone would misstate e0 by several tenths of a year.

Interpreting the controls

The basis toggle switches the sex-specific parameter set (the "Both" option is a birth-weighted blend). The valuation year slider walks the improvement surface forward or backward from the 2019 anchor and re-runs the life table, headline metrics, and every exhibit. Ratio and decomposition exhibits are shown on the selected single-sex basis where a blend would be uninformative.

Limitations & intended use

Figures are realistic but synthetic and are intended for discussion and framing, not for pricing or reserving. The model is a period representation and does not resolve genuine cohort (year-of-birth) effects, socioeconomic or smoker/non-smoker segmentation, or selection and duration effects in insured versus population lives — all of which a production basis must address. Before any downstream use, replace the synthetic surface with the relevant graduated base table and improvement scale (or the portfolio's own experience study).
